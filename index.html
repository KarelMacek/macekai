Study “AI Tools in Society: Impacts on Cognitive Offloading and the Future of Critical Thinking” by Michael Gerlich, Center for Strategic Corporate Foresight and Sustainability, SBS Swiss Business School — says something every AI professional should take seriously.

AI may not only replace people. It may slowly weaken the people who use it most.

The study found that frequent AI tool use was linked to higher cognitive offloading — people moving more of their thinking work onto technology. And higher cognitive offloading was linked to weaker critical thinking.

Here is the uncomfortable part for AI professionals:

You may be at higher risk than most.

Because you use AI more. You trust yourself around AI more. You can explain why the output looks reasonable. You can move faster than others. And because of that, you may notice the damage later.

But the damage can hit exactly where your value is built: judgment, problem framing, technical taste, deep understanding, and the ability to see when a beautiful answer is wrong.

The real danger is not “Will AI replace me?”

The deeper danger is: “Will AI slowly weaken my ability to build, evaluate, and lead AI well?”

The tool that makes AI professionals faster today may make them less valuable tomorrow — not because AI gets better, but because AI quietly stops them from training the mind needed to build good AI.

High ability has a hidden cost.

One more paper. One more tool. One more framework. One more meeting. One more opportunity. One more must-have direction.

At first it feels exciting. Then one day you look up — a dozen tabs open, a calendar that has no white space — and still the feeling that you are always behind, and never quite enough.

Something keeps pulling you forward — but not toward anything you actually chose. Work, wealth, family, health, and calm start pulling in different directions. You keep performing, but life starts to feel less like yours.

The question is not whether you can do more. The question is what kind of life all this ability is actually building.

FAQ

How does coaching actually work?

Sessions happen regularly — usually 3 or 12, depending on what you need. The method is brain-based coaching from the NeuroLeadership Institute: a structured way of working with how your brain actually makes sense of complex situations, decisions, pressure, and change.

Picture a focused 75-minute video call. No generic templates. Your thinking gets navigated — clearly, calmly, directly — toward the progress you defined. Each session ends with one concrete action you take before the next, because real change happens in the weeks between sessions, not only in the conversation.

What results can you actually expect?

The results depend on you — and that is the point. The real investment is not only the coaching fee. It is the work you do between sessions: the actions you define, commit to, and follow through on.

When income growth is the explicit focus, a 20% or more salary or revenue increase within a year is not unusual. An ROI of 400% or more within six months is realistic for the right person with the right goal.

But the outcomes most clients value even more are not financial: clearer direction, better decisions, and a life where your professional ambition and your personal life are no longer in conflict.

What makes this different from other coaching?

Three worlds that are rarely combined — and here they are in one process.

First: brain-based coaching from the NeuroLeadership Institute, grounded in how your mind actually works under pressure, uncertainty, and change. Second: structured problem-solving tools — TRIZ, Six Sigma, design thinking — for when the challenge needs more than reflection. Third: a deeper human layer, rooted in philosophy, anthropology, and the science of personal development.

You get structured thinking, human depth, and real-world action — not as separate modules, but woven into one conversation.

You will get no advice from your coach. Why?

You have probably already read the book, watched the talk, and received the good advice. What changed?

The goal is not to give you answers — it is to help you find the ones you will actually act on. You do not need someone to win the battle for you. You need to win it yourself, with a clear mind and a structured process at your side.

Advice is available when it genuinely helps — and it is welcome when it does. But it is the exception, not the foundation. The foundation is your own thinking: cleared, focused, and pointed at what actually matters.
